### **A Refugee in the Canopy**

This spring, a colony of bees built a hive in a tree on our farm. Then one day, a cat climbed the same tree.

She didn't climb it out of curiosity. She climbed it because it was her last resort. A pack of dogs was chasing her.

She sat there, a couple of feet away from the massive beehive, constantly looking down to see if the dogs had left her alone. They hadn't. There were three of them. They formed a circle around the tree and kept looking at her, reading her body language, making sure they didn't miss her if she jumped down.

This episode lasted half a day. She went up in the morning, already hungry and thirsty. By late afternoon, the dogs still hadn't left. She was outnumbered, exhausted, and started meowing, hoping for some help from the humans next door.

### **What the Bees Knew**

Here is the part that stopped me in my tracks.

She chose to sit mere feet away from a beehive that could have attacked her at any moment. But the bees didn't mind her at all.

Bees have collective intelligence. They didn't sense any danger when the cat sat beside them for hours. Her anxiety and fear were real, but they were not directed at the beehive. Somehow, the bees seemed to know that.

Probably they know honey is not the cat's food. Or perhaps they are intelligent enough to sense that the animal beside them was a refugee, not a predator. Someone who needed a safe space to lie low, not a threat to their colony.

This was pure nature protecting nature. No misunderstanding. No bee came over and attacked for no reason.

If you fear a beehive, it is probably because you know who the predator is. And so does the bee colony.

*FYI, this actually happened in my backyard. We eventually managed to give the cat a safe passage.*

### **Living With Beehives**

I have been living with beehives for a long time. It is not intentional, and it is not accidental either.

If you have a garden with a native variety of flowers and fruits, it will attract bees, and it is an entire ecosystem in motion. Bees feed on the nectar of flowers and budding fruits; they pollinate them so they turn into fruits and vegetables. They need some water or moist mud to feed on.

Eventually, they find a high vantage point, build their colony, and nest there for years, without getting in your way.

There are times when you see bees lose their way back to the hive because of electric lights and bulbs. If you are mindful enough not to keep outdoor lights on during twilight, they are not lost as much.

### **What You Can Do If You Spot a Hive Close By**

1. **Look at it and smile.** It means there is a thriving ecosystem nearby that is supporting their colony.

2. **Maintain distance. Let them be.** You don't have to poke them, smoke them, or disturb them physically.

3. **Keep a shallow plate of water** somewhere in the area, not close to the hive, somewhere you can easily access it to refill.

4. **Make a small pond in a container** with some guppy fish and floating plants. It provides moisture for the bees and becomes a micro-ecosystem of its own.

![Garden container pond with guppy fish and floating plants](/stories/garden_container_pond.png)

5. **Switch on outdoor lights only after it is fully dark**, so bees have sufficient time to return to their hive and aren't misled by brightness. Premature light seems to confuse them into thinking it is still daytime and disorients them.

6. **If a hive is in an unsafe spot**, sprinkle cinnamon powder near it, and they will relocate on their own, gently and without conflict.

### **Fear Is the Real Problem**

I have seen people get very scared at the mere mention of beehives. They immediately get nervous and want to get rid of them, even when the hive is not anywhere near them.

Bees play a crucial role in keeping us fed. We cannot afford to see them as threats. Every time we destroy a hive out of panic, we lose something that took nature enormous effort to build.

The best way forward is to educate yourself and not panic. There is no bliss in ignorance.

**Bee aware. Bee sensible.**
