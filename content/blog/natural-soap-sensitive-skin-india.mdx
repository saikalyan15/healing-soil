## What commercial soap does to sensitive skin

Most commercial soap bars sold in India - including many positioned as gentle, moisturising, or dermatologist-recommended - contain sodium lauryl sulfate. SLS is a synthetic detergent that creates lather fast and removes oils efficiently. This is why it appears in so many everyday bath products.

For some skin types, that fast-foam wash can feel too strong. The bar may leave skin feeling tight after bathing, especially when combined with hard water, heat, or frequent washing.

If your skin prefers mild products, the practical move is to choose a simpler bar first before adding more steps to the routine.

Commercial soap also frequently contains synthetic fragrance, listed as "fragrance" or "parfum" without further detail. If you prefer a quieter routine, choose bars scented with botanicals or essential oils, or choose an unscented option where available.

## Hard water makes everything feel stronger

Most Indian metros run on hard borewell or mixed-supply water. Bangalore, Chennai, Hyderabad, and large parts of Delhi and Pune all sit in the hard-to-very-hard range for much of the year.

Hard water matters for sensitive skin because minerals in the water react with soap and rinse away less cleanly. The same bar feels harsher in Bangalore than it does in Goa. If your skin feels tight after bathing in one city and fine in another, the water is often the variable, not your skin.

This is worth knowing before you blame a soap, and it is also why base choice matters more in hard-water cities. A creamier goat milk bar compensates for what hard water takes away. ## What "natural" actually means on a soap label

The word natural is not regulated in India for cosmetics. Any brand can use it regardless of what is in the product.

What to actually look for on the label:

- **No SLS or SLES** - sodium lauryl sulfate or sodium laureth sulfate
- **No parabens** - listed as methylparaben, propylparaben, butylparaben
- **No synthetic fragrance** - if "fragrance" or "parfum" appears without further detail, it is synthetic
- **No PEG compounds** - polyethylene glycol derivatives, often listed as PEG followed by a number

A bar that avoids all of these is genuinely different from most commercial soap, even if it does not use the word natural anywhere on the packaging.

## How to read a soap ingredients list

Soap labels in India follow the same descending-order rule as food labels: highest-quantity ingredient listed first.

SLS, if present, usually appears in the top five ingredients. It is a primary ingredient, not a trace. Look for it there first.

Handmade soap bases often appear under INCI names - the international standard for cosmetic ingredient naming. Coconut oil appears as "cocos nucifera oil." Shea butter as "butyrospermum parkii butter." Glycerin as "glycerin." These are fine - they are simply the standardised Latin-origin names for real ingredients.

The sulfate compounds to avoid - sodium lauryl sulfate, sodium laureth sulfate, sodium coco sulfate - use those exact words. If none appear in the list, the soap is at least SLS-free.

If there is no ingredient list at all, that is worth noting. Any responsible soap maker, handmade or otherwise, should tell you what is in the bar.

## Which base suits sensitive skin

Once you have found an SLS-free option without synthetic fragrance, the base matters.

**[Glycerin](/ingredient/glycerin)** is a byproduct of soap-making that commercial manufacturers typically extract and sell separately because it is valuable on its own. In a handmade bar, it stays in. Glycerin draws moisture into skin as you wash. It is gentle enough for most skin types. It suits oily or combination skin well, and is a reasonable starting point for mild sensitivity.

**[Goat milk](/ingredient/goat-milk)** replaces water in the soap base. It gives the bar a particularly creamy, gentle lather and a softer after-wash feel. For sensitive, dry, or combination skin types, goat milk is usually the most versatile base and the easiest place to start.

**[Shea butter](/ingredient/shea-butter)** is the richest of the three. It gives the bar a dense, conditioning feel during washing. It is a good fit when skin feels very dry or tight after showering, but it can feel too rich for oily skin.

## What to expect when you switch

If you switch from commercial soap to an SLS-free bar, the first week can feel different.

The lather may feel different from commercial soap. Handmade bars usually produce a creamier, quieter lather rather than a large synthetic foam.

The after-wash feel may also be different. Instead of a squeaky finish, an SLS-free bar should leave skin feeling clean, soft, and comfortable.

A soap is a daily wash product, not a medicine. What switching does reliably is simplify daily contact with synthetic additives. For many people, that alone changes how their bath routine feels day to day.
